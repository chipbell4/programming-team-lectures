Two Important Operations (2D & 3D)

Dot Product

The dot product $p_1 \cdot p_2$ is defined as $x_1x_2 + y_1y_2$. Fun facts:

  • $p \cdot p = \|p\|^2$
  • If $p_1 \cdot p_2 = 0$, then $p_1$ and $p_2$ are said to be orthogonal, which means they're at right angles with each other. This actually extends to vectors of any dimension and even to functions (a function is orthogonal to another)
  • $p_1 \cdot p_2 = \|p_1\|\|p_2\| cos(\theta)$, where $\theta$ is the angle between the two vectors.

Cross Product

For 2D, the cross product is defined as $p_1 \times p_2 = x_1y_2 - x_2y_1$. In 3D it's trickier, but I like this formula.

Cross products really only work in 3D, but we can fake 2D by pretending that $z=0$ for our vectors. In that case, the cross product returns a vector sticking out in the $z$ direction. In most 2D applications, we only care about the magnitude of the result anyways.

Cross Product (cont.)

Considering only 2D,

  • $p_1 \times p_2$ in 2D gives the volume of the parallelogram with side from the origin to $p_1$ and $p_2$.
  • As a consequence $\frac{1}{2}$ that will give the triangle area (very handy)
  • $p_1 \times p_2 = \|p_1\|\|p_2\|sin(\theta)\mathbf{n}$ where $\theta$ is the angle between the vectors, and $\mathbf{n}$ is the normal vector to the two (the direction of the cross product). Using this handy notation for calculating angle, we can always determine if two points are collinear ($\theta = 0$) because the cross product is 0.

Line Segments

Essentially, it's a continuum of points between two points. A nice way to write this is $\mathscr{l}(t) = p_0 + t(p_1 - p_0)$, where $0 \le t \le 1$. Worth noting, if $t$ is outside the range, the function is not really defined (unless you want it to be).

What sort of operations might we care about?

Intersection with another line segment?

Essentially set the two lines equal to each other, and fiddle with the algebra:

$\mathscr{l_1}(s) = p_0 + s(p_1 - p_0) = v_0 + t(v_1 - v_0) = \mathscr{l_2}(t)$:

We've got two free variables $s$ and $t$. But, if we cross-product both sides with $v_1 - v_0$ or $p_1 - p_0$, either the $s$ or $t$ term will cancel, and we'll be left with only 1 variable, which we can then solve for.

Intersection with other shapes?

Same approach, parameterize and set equal. See if you can cancel something out. Make sure to try dot or cross to cancel out a term.
